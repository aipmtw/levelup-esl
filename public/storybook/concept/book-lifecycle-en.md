# Book lifecycle — repeat purchases over a child's growth

## The insight
A child is not a one-shot customer. **One kid, followed through their 0–12 years, is worth many books** — not one. The same partner who sold (or gifted) the first book when the kid was 1 year old can keep selling matched-to-stage books for the next 10+ years.

This changes the economics from **one-off sales** to a **long relationship**. Partners who invest in a customer early earn repeatedly as the kid grows. Mark's per-generation revenue from that partner compounds quietly over time.

Amy's case is the clearest illustration, but the pattern applies to Audrey and Arita too.

## Amy's customer, illustrated

Take any one of Amy's new-parent friends, call her Ya-chen. Ya-chen has a 1-year-old daughter. Over the next 10 years, Amy's relationship with Ya-chen might look like this:

| Ya-chen's kid age | Amy generates and Ya-chen buys (or receives as gift) |
|---|---|
| **1yo** (first buy) | *First-words* book — body parts, family, basic objects. zh-TW only. Very simple. |
| 1yo+3mo | *My Day* routine book — morning, meals, bath, bedtime. zh-TW only. |
| 1yo+6mo | *Animals I Love* — pairs with Ya-chen's trip to the zoo. zh-TW only. |
| 1yo+9mo | *Ya-chen's first birthday* keepsake — a milestone book, zh-TW only, forever-treasured. |
| **2yo** (level up) | Same themes as before but with **simple English added** alongside zh-TW. "Dog / 狗狗". Bilingual exposure starts. |
| **2yo+6mo** (hero debut) | **Hero story** — a designed story where Ya-chen's daughter is the main character. "小美的海邊冒險 / Mei's Beach Adventure". Bilingual. |
| 3yo | New hero story on a different theme. Vocabulary slightly wider. |
| **4yo** (tier transition 0–3 → 4–6) | First 10–12 page book with real plot. Bilingual, longer sentences, richer vocabulary. |
| 5yo | Hero story with moral / theme ("sharing", "courage"). |
| 6yo | First pre-school-entry book. Optionally adds Japanese as third language if Ya-chen's household is multilingual. |
| ... | ...continuing through 7–9 and 10–12 tiers, each with longer books, richer text, more sophisticated plots. |

**Over 10 years, Ya-chen might end up with 15–25 books from Amy.** Not one.

## The three axes of growth

When a partner generates the Nth book for a returning customer, they can grow the product along **three independent axes**:

### Axis 1 — topic / book type
- **0–3 book types:** first-words, daily routine, sensory (colors/sounds), animals, family, milestone keepsakes.
- **4–6 book types:** hero adventures, themed exploration (vehicles, nature), simple emotional stories (sharing, bravery), short bedtime stories.
- **7–9 book types:** chapter-lite hero adventures, topic deep-dives (dinosaurs, space, oceans), character-driven narratives.
- **10–12 book types:** multi-chapter stories, genre fiction (mystery, fantasy), vocabulary-focused books, non-fiction on kid's interest.

Partners can rotate across book types to keep the series fresh — the same kid might get a new milestone book at their birthday, a new hero adventure at summer holiday, and a new routine book when they start kindergarten. Each feels different even though they share the main character.

### Axis 2 — **the kid as hero**
A core upgrade path: move from **generic topic books** ("colors", "animals") to **stories where the kid is literally the protagonist**.

- **Entry** — "Mei likes dogs" (kid is mentioned, topic is the point).
- **Middle** — "Mei visits the farm" (kid is on-screen, topic is the setting).
- **Full hero** — "Mei's Dragon Adventure" (kid drives the plot, overcomes something, is the center of the narrative).

Hero books have **much higher emotional attachment** — kids recognize themselves and want to re-read, parents treasure them as keepsakes. They justify a higher list price and they generate word-of-mouth faster ("look, my kid is in this book"). Partners should introduce hero books around age 2 and lean into them heavily from 3 onward.

### Axis 3 — **language level-up over time**
As the kid grows, the language in the book grows with them. Same partner, same child, same underlying engine — new book at a higher level.

| Child age | Typical language config | Sentence complexity |
|---|---|---|
| 0–1 | zh-TW only | 1 word or 1 short phrase per page |
| 1–2 | zh-TW only | 1 short sentence per page |
| 2–3 | zh-TW + basic English (pair labels) | 1 sentence + English word pairings |
| 3–4 | zh-TW + English (sentence-level) | 1–2 sentences per page, both languages |
| 4–5 | zh-TW + English (+ optional ja) | 2–3 sentences, simple dialogue |
| 5–7 | zh-TW + English (+ optional ja) | Short paragraphs, story arc |
| 7–9 | zh-TW + English (+ optional ja) | Multi-paragraph pages, chapter-lite |
| 9–12 | zh-TW + English (+ optional ja) | Full chapters, richer vocabulary, genre variety |

**Critical:** the level-up is done by **generating a new book at the new level**, not by editing an old book. The 1-year-old book stays as a precious archive. The 2-year-old book is a fresh new thing on the parent's shelf. The series grows, the old books are never thrown away.

## How the platform supports this

### Per-kid history in the partner's private workspace
Each end-user kid in the partner's workspace has a profile:
- name, age (auto-advances over time)
- **book history** — every book ever generated for this kid, with date, tier, theme, language mix, level-up markers
- **next suggestions** — when the kid's age advances into a new tier, the workspace nudges the partner: "Mei is now 4. Want to generate her first 4–6 tier book? Based on her history, suggested themes: hero adventure (her favorite), animals (she had one already at age 2, time for a richer version)."
- **milestone reminders** — birthdays, back-to-school, Lunar New Year, etc. Trigger partner to proactively reach out with a new keepsake book suggestion.

### Book type taxonomy
The platform tags every generated book with:
- `tier` — 0-3 / 4-6 / 7-9 / 10-12
- `type` — first-words / routine / sensory / animals / hero / milestone / themed / story / chapter / ...
- `theme` — free-text theme the partner chose
- `languages` — zh-TW / zh-TW+en / zh-TW+en+ja
- `complexity_level` — 1–10 scale within the tier

Partners use these tags to find gaps ("this kid has never had a hero book") and to plan the next book.

### Level-up markers
When a partner generates a new book for a returning kid, the platform auto-suggests the appropriate complexity_level based on the kid's age + prior book history. Partner can always override. When complexity_level jumps significantly, the book is marked in the workspace as a **level-up book** — a note-worthy progression event the partner can celebrate with the parent ("your daughter just levelled up! This one has full sentences in English").

## Revenue implication for each partner tier

- **Starter (no sub-partners, casual use):** a partner with 10 end-user kids in their circle, each getting 1–2 books per year, generates 10–20 books/year. Low tier volume, nice side-income.
- **Growth (small network):** a partner with 30 end-user kids (their own circle + a few recruited sub-partners' circles), each getting 2–3 books per year, generates 60–90 books/year. Meaningful steady business.
- **Pro (full master mode with sub-partners):** a master partner like Audrey with her own circle + 5 sub-partners each with their own circle, aggregating 150 end-user kids, each getting 2–3 books per year → **300–450 books/year** flowing through the platform under one master's tier. Per-generation infra fee × 400 books = genuinely interesting revenue for Mark, real business for Audrey.

The lifecycle model is what makes the Pro tier **worth it**. Without repeat purchases, a master partner's book count would plateau; with repeat purchases tied to kids growing up, volume grows year over year even if partner doesn't add new customers.

## What this means for M1

M1 does not need the full lifecycle tooling. M1 needs:
- **The ability to generate a book for a returning kid** without having to re-enter all the kid's info (per-kid profile in partner workspace).
- **Basic book-type tags** on generated books (tier, primary theme, languages, complexity_level).
- **Simple book history view** in the partner workspace (list of books per kid, sorted by date).

M2 adds the suggestion engine, milestone reminders, level-up markers, and richer catalog templates.

## What this means for the three partners' messaging

- **Amy:** "Your 1-year-old's first books are the beginning of a library. Each year I'll make new ones that grow with him." Lifetime relationship from day one.
- **Audrey:** "I've done this for my own 6-year-old and my own 10-year-old. Every year, new books at the new level. Your kid's series will keep growing." Credibility via her two demo arcs.
- **Arita:** "Every month I see your child at our lesson. Every month I can make a book that matches exactly what we're working on this term." The ultimate recurring teaching aid.

All three partners sell a **relationship**, not a single book. The book is the deliverable; the relationship is the product.
