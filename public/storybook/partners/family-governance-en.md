# Family governance — Amy is Mark's daughter

## The situation
One of the three master partners, **Amy**, is Mark's daughter. Her son (Mark's grandson) is the first real end user of the platform. This is a strength — family trust is unbeatable, dogfood feedback is immediate, and the emotional product story is genuine. It also creates boundaries that need to be handled explicitly, not quietly.

This doc is written so that if Audrey or Arita ever reads it, the relationship is visible, the guardrails are clear, and there's no quiet favoritism that might make them feel second-class.

## Principles

### 1. Family relationship is disclosed, not hidden
- Amy is identified in `partners/amy-en.md` as "Mark's daughter" openly.
- Other master partners know Amy is family.
- No one is surprised later.

### 2. Same platform terms as other master partners
- Amy onboards through the same flow as Audrey and Arita.
- Same CMS, same private workspace, same PWA tooling, same review gate, same Luce credits.
- No platform shortcuts, no hidden admin features, no special-case code branches for "the boss's daughter".
- **Internal rule:** if a future partner asks "does Amy get X", the answer must be "Amy gets exactly what you get".

### 3. Billing is real, even if family
- Amy is billed like any master partner — per-generation infra fee with tiers, see [`../pricing/partner-terms-en.md`](../pricing/partner-terms-en.md).
- Mark may choose to **apply a credit to her account** (family gift, not a pricing discount). The invoice still shows the real number so that usage data and tier-upgrade signals are realistic.
- Mark must **not** run Amy "off the books" — no informal freebies that leave no trail. That creates ambiguity if Amy ever grows big enough to bring her own sub-partners onto their own billing.
- **Test:** if Audrey saw Amy's account, would she feel fairly treated? Keep the answer yes.

### 4. Mark does not snoop in Amy's private workspace
- Mark has `global_admin` override on the platform — technically he can see every partner's private workspace.
- **For Amy specifically**, Mark commits to the same norm as for Audrey and Arita: only use global admin for legitimate support reasons, never for casual browsing.
- **Specifically forbidden (even though technically possible):**
  - Browsing his grandson's books without Amy's explicit request.
  - Reading Amy's customer list or pricing notes out of curiosity.
  - Checking Amy's review notes to "see what she thinks".
- **Specifically allowed (with Amy's consent):**
  - Amy hands Mark a specific book to look at ("Dad, is page 3 weird?").
  - Amy explicitly asks Mark to debug an issue with her workspace.
  - Emergency support where Amy is unreachable and a sub-partner of hers needs help.
- Every global admin access to Amy's workspace is **logged** and Amy can see the log in her own workspace.

### 5. The grandson's content is Amy's, not Mark's
- The books generated for Mark's grandson are Amy's customer data, not family property.
- Mark does **not** post them publicly, share them, or use them in marketing **without Amy's explicit approval** — and even with approval, the grandson's real name, face, or distinctive details should be scrubbed unless Amy specifically opts in.
- Same rule as any other parent's child on the platform. Family status doesn't erase privacy.

### 6. Conflict of interest on product decisions
- Amy's feedback is invaluable but her voice is not louder than Audrey's or Arita's.
- Product decisions that affect all partners go through the same process regardless of who raised them.
- If Amy asks for a feature that only helps her 0–3 tier at the cost of the 4–12 tier, Mark applies the same "does it serve the whole platform" test he'd apply to any partner.
- **Soft rule:** when Amy advocates for something, Mark consciously asks himself "would I say yes if Audrey asked for this?". If the answer is no, the answer is no.

### 7. Business boundary from family boundary
- Disagreements about the business are resolved through **business channels** (partner agreements, clear terms, written decisions), not family dynamics.
- If Amy wants to quit as a partner, she quits cleanly — Mark does not guilt her. The platform access and customer data are hers to keep.
- If Mark ever wants to end Amy's partner access (can't imagine the scenario, but for completeness), it follows the same process as with any other partner.
- Family holidays are family holidays. Do not use Sunday lunch to dictate a platform roadmap change.

### 8. Future family partners
- If Mark's wife, other children, siblings, etc., ever want to become partners, they onboard through the same process. Same principles apply to all of them.
- The plan should never have "founder family special tier" as a concept.

## What this buys Mark

- **Clean relationship with non-family partners.** Audrey and Arita can look at the plan and see Amy's being treated fairly. No whispers.
- **Clean relationship with his daughter.** Business disagreements don't poison the family. Family issues don't distort the business.
- **A real, not theatrical, dogfood loop.** Amy gives honest feedback because she trusts the boundaries are clear and she's not being watched over.
- **A story Mark can tell without flinching.** "My daughter is my first partner, my grandson is my first user, and here's exactly how I handle that" is a strong founder narrative. Secrecy would undercut it.
