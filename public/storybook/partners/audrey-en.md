# Partner — Audrey (working-parent / peer-community channel) — **master partner**

> Audrey is one of Mark's **two direct master partners**. She can recruit and coach sub-partners under her umbrella via the master-partner model. See [`../concept/master-partner-model-en.md`](../concept/master-partner-model-en.md). Mark bills Audrey; Audrey bills her sub-partners however she wants.

## Who she is
Audrey is a **professional full-time working mother** with two kids: a **6-year-old** and a **10-year-old**. She lives the target market every day — she IS the customer, not just selling to it.

Her specific life stage matters: she is **past the baby phase**, deep in the **elementary-school-parenting phase**, and her social circles reflect that — she is not hanging out with new moms in baby groups, she is hanging out with **other working parents of school-age kids**.

## Authentic age-tier coverage
Audrey directly lives the **4–6** tier (her 6-year-old) and the **10–12** tier (her 10-year-old), and is a credible voice on the **7–9** tier because her 6yo is aging into it and her 10yo came from it.

## Target
**Children aged 4–12** in her parent social circles. Lower end (0–3) is not her market — that's Amy's specialty.

- Her own **LINE group** of fellow working parents is the primary community hub.
- Personal demos: she generates a book for her 6-year-old AND a book for her 10-year-old. Two demo works side by side demonstrate the age-tier range she credibly serves. Friends with similar-aged kids see which one matches their own child.

## Social status / life stage positioning
Audrey's credibility comes from being **a peer in the same life stage as her customers**. Her market is other full-time-working mothers (and fathers) of elementary-age kids — time-poor, income-stable, invested in their kids' bilingual education, active in school-parent LINE groups. She is not positioning as an expert; she is positioning as "one of us who found something that works".

## The show-off-at-school dynamic (unique to Audrey's channel)
Elementary-age kids (4–12) **love to show things off to their classmates**. A personalized book where the kid is the literal hero is **social capital** at school:

- **At recess:** the kid pulls out their tablet / phone, opens the PWA ("look, this book is ABOUT me"), and passes it around. Classmates crowd around.
- **At show-and-tell:** the physical printed version (from the `.zip` bundle) is brought to school as "my favorite book". Teacher may even read it to the whole class.
- **At birthday parties:** a hero-adventure book as a personalized gift beats a generic toy. Other parents at the party notice.

This creates a **viral loop Audrey gets for free**:

```
Audrey makes hero book for her 6yo
   ↓
Her 6yo shows classmates at school
   ↓
Classmate: "I want one too!"
   ↓
Classmate's parent (in Audrey's school-parent LINE group) asks Audrey
   ↓
New customer — same age tier, same community, zero ad spend
```

**This dynamic is unique to Audrey's channel.** Amy's 1-year-old cannot show off to anyone (wrong age). Arita's students don't show off teaching-aids — those are quiet, home-bound tools. But Audrey's 6yo and 10yo specifically **want** to show off a book that makes them the hero.

### What this means for Audrey's product design
- **Lean into hero books early.** Every one of Audrey's recurring customers should have at least one hero-adventure book per year. That's the one that spreads.
- **Design for show-off-ability:**
  - Big, impressive illustrations (fits the 4–12 art style anyway).
  - Kid's name on the cover in large, obvious letters.
  - A "cool" storyline — adventure, mystery, superhero, dragon — not a routine book.
  - Kid's unique distinguishing trait worked into the story (plays soccer, loves space, has a cat named Momo) so classmates recognize it's really about them.
- **Offer the printed-book SKU:** the `.zip` + print-on-demand is especially valuable for Audrey's tier because a physical book can be carried to school. PWA alone is passed around less publicly than a hardback.
- **Price the hero books at the upper end of the list-price range.** Show-off books generate downstream sales — they're worth more per unit.

## Audrey as a growth engine for Mark
Because of the show-off dynamic, **Audrey's channel has the highest organic-growth multiplier of the three master partners**. Every hero book she sells doesn't just get one customer — it gets seen by a classroom's worth of potential new customers. If Audrey sells 50 hero books in a year, that's 50 books × ~25 classmates viewed each = ~1,250 kid-impressions, many of which reach other school parents.

Audrey is worth investing in heavily during M1 because her channel's per-book marketing efficiency beats the other two.
